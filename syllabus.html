Course Overview

What is Introductory Biology?

Biology is the science of life—the study of everything from microscopic molecules to entire ecosystems. This course is designed to give you a strong foundation in the core concepts of biology so that you're ready to dive deeper into more advanced topics later in your college career. Along the way, you'll start to see how these concepts connect, giving you a greater appreciation for the complexity (and beauty!) of living systems.

Our goals are simple: to help you build confidence as a scientist, to give you the tools you'll need for future courses, and—most importantly—to spark your curiosity. Biology is full of fascinating questions, and we hope this course helps you discover the excitement of exploring them.

Course Format

This course is taught online via asynchronous instruction through the course Canvas page, with exams taken via Measure Learning/ProctorU or scheduled by you at a registered testing center.

What to Call Your Instructor

At the college level, particularly in the biological sciences, your instructors all have terminal degrees (the highest possible degree in our field: Ph.D.). It is appropriate to call us Dr. or Professor. You may call me Dr. Wiggins or Professor Wiggins.

My Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy for concurrent enrollment courses centers on the idea that carefully designed learning materials can create a rigorous, engaging, and equitable learning environment even when students interact with the content outside of a traditional university classroom.

Instructional videos are typically short — generally under ten minutes — to support attention and reduce unnecessary cognitive load. Each video is accompanied by clear guidance about what students should focus on while watching. By explicitly identifying the key ideas or questions students should be considering, I help direct attention toward the most important conceptual relationships rather than passive viewing.

Assigned readings are organized around specific topics rather than traditional textbook chapters, allowing students to focus on the most relevant concepts. This approach reflects my broader view of learning as an active cognitive process. Students develop durable understanding when they revisit challenging ideas, make connections across topics, and apply biological principles to new situations.

Ultimately, my goal is to create a learning environment that maintains the rigor and expectations of a university biology class while providing students with the structure and clarity needed to engage deeply with the material.

Learning Objectives

After completing this course, students will be able to:

  1. Explain how scientific knowledge is generated and evaluated so that they can interpret biological claims, distinguish evidence-based conclusions from unsupported assertions, and critically evaluate scientific information in everyday life.
  2. Design biological investigations and interpret experimental data so that they can evaluate hypotheses, analyze graphs and datasets, and draw evidence-based conclusions about biological processes.
  3. Explain how the structure of biological molecules, cells, and systems relates to their function so that they can predict how changes in structure (such as mutations, environmental changes, or disease) influence biological outcomes.
  4. Describe how genetic information is stored, transmitted, and expressed in living organisms so that they can explain how traits arise, how mutations generate variation, and how genetic information influences phenotype.
  5. Explain how cell division and inheritance mechanisms generate genetic diversity so that they can predict patterns of inheritance and understand how meiosis, recombination, and fertilization contribute to variation in populations.
  6. Explain the mechanisms of evolutionary change so that they can interpret how mutation, natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and speciation shape biological diversity across generations.
  7. Trace the flow of energy and matter through biological systems so that they can explain how processes such as photosynthesis and cellular respiration support life.
  8. Explain how biological systems coordinate processes to maintain function so that they can analyze how disruptions (such as disease, toxins, or environmental stress) affect organismal health and survival.
  9. Analyze how organisms interact with one another and with their environment so that they can interpret population dynamics, ecological relationships, and ecosystem stability.
  10. Evaluate how human activities influence biological systems so that they can understand how actions such as habitat modification, resource use, and technological innovation affect ecosystems and global biological processes.

Embedded Learning Questions (ELQs)

Each instructional video contains embedded learning questions. These are designed to highlight important points and ensure you are engaged. ELQs can be retaken an unlimited number of times before the due date.

Important: Just because a concept does not have an accompanying embedded learning question does not mean it is unimportant or will not be tested.

Take-Home Assignments (HW)

Throughout the semester, assignments intended to help you process the most challenging material will be assigned. More specifics will be provided for each assignment. All assignments are submitted through Canvas.

Writing Assignments

There are two writing assignments over the course of the semester:

  • Interpreting a Figure — Look at a figure from a peer-reviewed article and identify key components including the dependent and independent variables.
  • Writing an Abstract — Write a scientific abstract for a paper based on a figure from that paper. Your abstract should be consistent with what you can learn from the figure; it does not have to match the actual paper's abstract.

Tests

There are three tests during the semester, each worth 150 points. Tests are taken via Measure Learning/ProctorU or in person at a certified testing center — self-scheduled by you within the test window. Tests will be available alongside regular module content during their respective weeks.

Missed test policy: If you miss Test 1, 2, or 3 for any reason, notify your instructor within 48 hours and your Final Exam grade will be used for both the final and the missed test. This policy applies to one missed test only.

Final Exam

The final exam is comprehensive and worth 200 points. It is taken via Measure Learning/ProctorU or at a certified testing center during finals week.

AI Acceptable Use

Artificial intelligence (AI) tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Copilot, etc.) may be used only as a learning aid to support your understanding of biology concepts. All homework and writing assignments must be completed independently and in your own voice.

✓ Permitted Uses

  • Explore or review general biology concepts covered in lecture, readings, or prior coursework
  • Ask for alternative explanations, analogies, or summaries of topics
  • Identify areas of confusion to guide further studying
  • Create practice higher-order thinking test questions
  • Help plan how to study or approach learning the material (not how to answer a specific question)

✗ Prohibited Uses

  • Answer, solve, or complete any homework question, in whole or in part
  • Paste, upload, paraphrase, or submit any homework prompt, question, dataset, figure, or scenario into an AI tool
  • Use AI to generate explanations, calculations, or written responses that directly address a homework question or writing assignment
  • Use AI-generated language, reasoning, or structure as a template for your answers, even if you rewrite it

"In Your Own Voice" Requirement

All homework responses and writing assignments must be written in your own voice, reflecting your personal understanding of the material. Your voice should sound like a college freshman. You must be able to explain and defend your reasoning without reliance on external tools. Work that reflects AI-style phrasing, structure, or reasoning — rather than your own thinking — may be treated as unauthorized assistance.

AI may help you learn the material, but it may not help you produce the work.

Why This Policy Exists

Learning biology requires you to practice thinking through problems yourself. Homework and writing assignments are far less about getting the correct answer than about developing your ability to reason, explain biological processes, and apply concepts in your own words. If an AI tool does the thinking, structuring, or explaining for you, then you are not doing the learning. This policy exists to ensure that assignments support real understanding, prepare you for exams and future courses, and reflect your own developing scientific voice.

Use of AI beyond the limits described above constitutes unauthorized assistance and a violation of the academic integrity policy — including cases where AI use cannot be conclusively proven but submitted work shows strong evidence of external generation. If you are unsure whether a specific use is allowed, do not use it and ask before completing the assignment.
